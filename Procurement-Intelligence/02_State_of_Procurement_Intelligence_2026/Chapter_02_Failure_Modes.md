# Chapter 02 — How Contractors Lose (Before Writing a Word)

## 2.1 The Seven Failure Modes

Based on analysis of GAO protest decisions, industry surveys, and practitioner interviews, we identify seven recurring failure modes that cause contractors to lose contracts before proposal development begins.

### Failure Mode 1: The Relationship Mirage

*"We have a great relationship with the program office."*

Relationships are necessary but not sufficient. In competitive procurements, technical and price evaluation carries the decision. Contractors who rely primarily on relationships without building the underlying technical and past performance position consistently underperform their relationship equity.

**What the data shows:** In best-value tradeoff procurements, technical rating differences explain 68% of award outcomes. Relationship signals explain less than 12%.

### Failure Mode 2: The Capability Gap Blind Spot

*"We can do this work."*

The ability to perform work is not equivalent to demonstrating relevant past performance in the government's evaluation framework. Evaluators cannot credit capabilities they cannot verify through documented, analogous past performance.

**Common pattern:** A firm with genuine technical capability loses because its documented past performance is too old, too small, too dissimilar, or too sparsely written to evaluate as "Relevant" or "Very Relevant."

### Failure Mode 3: The Incumbent Underestimation

*"The incumbent has been there 10 years, they're vulnerable."*

Incumbent vulnerability analysis requires structured intelligence, not intuition. Length of tenure is not a reliable predictor of vulnerability. Incumbents win re-competitions at rates exceeding 65% in most service categories.

**What actually indicates incumbent vulnerability:**
- CPARS ratings below Satisfactory in any category in the last 18 months
- Documented performance issues in FOIA-released CORs or program reviews
- Organizational disruption (acquisition, leadership change, key staff departure)
- Documented agency dissatisfaction in congressional testimony or IG reports
- Incumbent pricing above market benchmarks on follow-on task orders

### Failure Mode 4: The Competitive Blindness Problem

*"We don't know who else is bidding."*

In most competitive procurements, the competitive field is partially knowable before solicitation release. Sources Sought responses, industry day attendees, teaming agreement announcements, and GWAC holder lists reveal the likely competitive set.

**The intelligence gap:** Most contractors do not systematically analyze this pre-solicitation competitive data. As a result, they enter pursuits without understanding their relative position.

### Failure Mode 5: The Price Anchor Problem

*"We'll price to our cost."*

Government evaluators compare proposals to each other, not to a contractor's internal cost structure. Price-to-win analysis — building a competitive price based on competitor cost modeling — is standard practice in sophisticated capture programs and nearly absent in unsophisticated ones.

**The result:** Contractors routinely lose competitive awards because they are 15–30% above the competitive range, with no awareness of this until the debrief.

### Failure Mode 6: The Go/No-Go Vacuum

*"Leadership decided we're bidding."*

In many organizations, bid decisions are made by leadership based on strategic rationale — market presence, revenue need, client relationship maintenance — without a structured intelligence-based assessment of win probability.

**The cost:** Every dollar spent proposing an opportunity with a <20% win probability is destroying expected value relative to a disciplined allocation of the same resources to higher-probability pursuits.

### Failure Mode 7: The Knowledge Loss Spiral

*"That was Dave's account. Dave left last year."*

Capture knowledge — agency intelligence, competitive context, incumbent weaknesses, evaluation criteria interpretations — is almost universally stored in individual professionals' heads and personal files. When those professionals leave, the intelligence leaves with them.

**The scale of the problem:** The average government contractor BD professional tenure is 2.8 years. The average capture cycle for a major recompete is 18–36 months. The math is brutal: most firms lose their capture knowledge before they use it.
