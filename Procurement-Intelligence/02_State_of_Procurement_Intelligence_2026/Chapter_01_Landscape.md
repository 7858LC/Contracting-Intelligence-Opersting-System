# Chapter 01 — The Procurement Intelligence Landscape

## 1.1 The Market We Are Describing

U.S. federal procurement alone exceeded $700 billion in fiscal year 2024. State and local governments add another $2+ trillion. Add international public procurement — EU, UK, multilateral development banks, Commonwealth nations — and the total public procurement market exceeds $10 trillion annually.

Every dollar of that procurement is won or lost before a proposal is written.

The proposal is a formality. The capture — the intelligence-driven strategy that precedes and shapes the proposal — is where contracts are won.

Yet the discipline of capture intelligence receives a fraction of the investment that proposal production receives.

## 1.2 The BD Spend Problem

The average government contractor spends 4–8% of revenue on business development. For a $50M firm, that is $2–4M annually in BD expense.

How is that money spent?

- **Relationship maintenance:** 30–40% (valuable, but not intelligence)
- **Proposal production:** 25–35% (downstream of the decision that matters)
- **Conferences and marketing:** 10–20% (brand visibility, not capture)
- **Actual intelligence analysis:** 5–15% (the work that determines win rates)

The industry spends the majority of its BD budget on activities that happen after the critical intelligence decisions have already been made — often with insufficient rigor.

## 1.3 The Information Asymmetry Problem

Government source selection evaluators know:
- Exactly what they want
- What past performance they consider relevant
- What price range they consider competitive
- Which incumbent weaknesses they want addressed
- Which technical approaches they find credible

Contractors typically know:
- What the solicitation says (which often obscures what evaluators actually want)
- Their own capabilities and past performance
- A surface-level understanding of the competitive field

This information asymmetry is structural. It is baked into the procurement process by design. The FAR requires government evaluators to maintain source selection sensitivity.

But the asymmetry is smaller than contractors assume. Decades of awards, debriefs, GAO protests, and FOIA releases have created a public record rich enough to substantially close the gap — for firms with the tools to read it.

## 1.4 What Procurement Intelligence Addresses

Procurement Intelligence does not eliminate information asymmetry. It compresses it — systematically, at scale, faster than human analysts can.

A mature Procurement Intelligence practice addresses:

1. **Market shaping intelligence** — Identifying opportunities 12–36 months before solicitation, when influence is still possible
2. **Pursuit entry intelligence** — Rapid synthesis of opportunity attractiveness, competitive landscape, and firm fit
3. **Capture phase intelligence** — Deep dives on agency priorities, incumbent weaknesses, and evaluation criteria
4. **Pre-proposal intelligence** — Award simulation, gap analysis, and price-to-win modeling
5. **Post-award intelligence** — Debrief analysis, loss pattern identification, and win rate measurement

Most contractors practice element 3 partially, element 4 inadequately, and elements 1, 2, and 5 barely at all.
